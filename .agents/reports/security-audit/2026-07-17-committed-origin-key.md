## Decision

The owner has accepted this risk and declined rotation. That is a human call, correctly
made by a human: `.agents/autonomy.md` forbids an agent from granting `accepted-risk` or
rotating a credential. No further action — do **not** re-raise this signature on future
sweeps unless a condition below changes.

**The cert is doing a real job, and the audit initially got this wrong.** The owner keeps
a DNS entry resolving `*.whistlebird.co.nz` to the local machine and develops against that
hostname rather than `localhost`, in order to exercise CORS, cookie scoping, and SameSite
behaviour that `localhost` cannot reproduce. Against that hostname the SAN matches
exactly, and the certificate is the correct one for the purpose. The key/CSR was
generated by the owner and signed by CloudFlare — "a cert I generated" is accurate.

The audit's first pass asserted the opposite (that the cert "cannot be silencing Chrome"
and that a self-signed `localhost` cert would "work better"). Both claims were wrong. See
"What the audit got wrong" below — it is the most useful part of this file.

## What the audit got wrong (the reusable part)

The first pass asserted that the cert "cannot be silencing Chrome on localhost" and that a
self-signed `localhost` cert "would work better". Both were wrong, and the way they were
wrong is the lesson:

- **The hostname claim was an inference, not a finding.** The audit read
  `BASE_URL = "https://localhost:8005"` in the test files and concluded the owner browses
  `localhost`. He doesn't: a DNS entry points `*.whistlebird.co.nz` at the local machine,
  the SAN matches, and there is no hostname mismatch. The evidence for the real setup was
  never in the repo — it is in DNS and a browser profile.
- **The "better alternative" was worse.** A self-signed `localhost` cert cannot exercise
  CORS, cookie scoping, or SameSite against a real domain. The recommendation would have
  broken the exact workflow the cert exists to support.
- **The discrepancy was a question, not an error.** The owner's account ("a local dev cert
  I generated") conflicted with the cert metadata (CloudFlare Origin CA, production
  wildcard). The audit resolved that conflict by assuming he was confused. He wasn't —
  he generated the key/CSR, CloudFlare signed it, and he uses it locally on purpose. The
  right move when the owner's account contradicts your evidence is to **ask**, because the
  owner routinely holds context the filesystem does not contain.

An audit that "corrects" the person who built the system, from partial evidence, spends
the credibility it needs for the finding that actually matters.

## Conditions that void this acceptance

Re-raise immediately if any becomes true — the key stays valid until **2028-01-14**:

- The repo gains **any** additional member, or is forked.
- The repo becomes **public** or is mirrored anywhere.
- A clone, backup, or CI artifact containing it leaves the owner's control.
- The key is deployed to, or reused by, the live origin (see open question).

## Open question (not blocking, worth knowing)

Whether the live origin currently serves this same key. If yes, an exposure would be
directly usable to impersonate the origin to CloudFlare for `*.whistlebird.co.nz`; if the
origin was reconfigured at some point, this copy is stale and the acceptance is even
safer. Nobody has checked, and nothing requires it to be checked — it only changes the
severity of a disclosure that has not happened. `ci/setup_server.sh:13-14` generates its
own self-signed cert, so CI does not depend on this key.

## No alternative proposed

The obvious-looking swap — a self-signed `localhost` cert — is **not** an improvement
here: it would break the CORS/cookie/SameSite testing the real hostname exists to enable.
The current setup is the right tool for the job. Anyone revisiting this should start from
that, not from "a private key in git is always wrong".
